BENZODIAZEPINE USE DISORDER TREATMENT

Acute Reversal — Flumazenil:
  Mechanism: competitive antagonist at the benzodiazepine binding site on GABA-A receptors
  Indication: reversal of acute benzodiazepine overdose in non-dependent patients
  Dose: 0.2 mg IV, may repeat to max 3-5 mg
  Half-life: 40-80 minutes (shorter than most benzodiazepines — re-sedation risk)
  CRITICAL WARNING: flumazenil can precipitate seizures in benzodiazepine-dependent patients. Use with extreme caution and only when respiratory support is unavailable. NOT recommended as routine reversal in chronic users.

Medically Supervised Taper (Primary Treatment):
No FDA-approved medications exist specifically for benzodiazepine use disorder. Gradual dose reduction is the standard of care:
  1. Ashton Manual protocol: convert to diazepam, taper 1-2 mg every 1-2 weeks
  2. Alternatively: taper the patient's current benzodiazepine at 10-25% every 1-2 weeks
  3. Inpatient detox for high-dose dependence (>100 mg diazepam equivalents/day)
  4. Phenobarbital substitution: alternative for patients who cannot tolerate diazepam taper

Adjunctive Medications During Taper:
  - Carbamazepine/valproate: anticonvulsant protection during taper
  - Gabapentin/pregabalin: anxiety and insomnia management (caution: own abuse potential)
  - SSRIs/SNRIs: treat underlying anxiety disorder that may have prompted benzodiazepine use
  - Hydroxyzine: non-addictive anxiolytic alternative
  - Propranolol: manages autonomic symptoms (tachycardia, tremor)

Psychosocial Interventions:
  - CBT for insomnia (CBT-I): addresses the insomnia that often drives benzodiazepine dependence
  - Motivational interviewing: enhances taper adherence
  - Gradual exposure therapy: for patients using benzodiazepines for anxiety disorders
  - Support groups: BenzoBuddies and similar peer support communities (often referenced in social media posts — RECOVERY stage indicator)

Social Media Signals for Recovery:
Mentions of "taper", "Ashton Manual", "switching to Valium", "cutting dose", "benzo-free" are strong Recovery stage indicators in the context of benzodiazepine dependence.
