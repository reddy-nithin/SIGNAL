BENZODIAZEPINE DRUG INTERACTIONS

Benzodiazepines + Opioids (FDA BLACK BOX WARNING):
In 2016, the FDA issued a black box warning for concurrent benzodiazepine-opioid prescribing. Combined CNS depression from GABA-A modulation (benzos) and mu-opioid receptor activation produces synergistic respiratory depression that is frequently fatal.
  - CDC data: benzodiazepines are co-involved in approximately 16% of opioid overdose deaths
  - Naloxone partially reverses the opioid component but does NOT reverse benzodiazepine effects
  - Flumazenil (benzodiazepine antagonist) is available but rarely used in overdose due to seizure precipitation risk in dependent patients
  - Risk is highest with short-acting, high-potency combinations (alprazolam + fentanyl)
  - Street drug context: "bars and blues" (Xanax + counterfeit oxycodone/fentanyl) is an extremely dangerous poly-drug pattern

Benzodiazepines + Alcohol:
Both modulate GABA-A receptors through different binding sites, producing additive-to-synergistic CNS depression. Combined use dramatically increases:
  - Respiratory depression risk
  - Amnesia/blackout risk
  - Fall and injury risk (especially elderly)
  - Aspiration risk
Cross-tolerance exists: alcoholics may require higher benzodiazepine doses for therapeutic effect, creating a narrow therapeutic window.

Benzodiazepines + Gabapentinoids (pregabalin, gabapentin):
Emerging concern. Both enhance GABAergic transmission (different mechanisms). Combined use increases sedation, respiratory depression, and overdose risk. FDA issued a warning in 2019.

CYP3A4 Interactions:
Benzodiazepines metabolized by CYP3A4 (alprazolam, midazolam, triazolam) have clinically significant interactions with:
  - Inhibitors (increase benzo levels): ketoconazole, itraconazole, clarithromycin, ritonavir, grapefruit juice
  - Inducers (decrease benzo levels): rifampin, carbamazepine, phenytoin
  Note: lorazepam and oxazepam bypass CYP3A4 and are preferred when drug interactions are a concern

Benzodiazepines + Z-drugs (zolpidem, zaleplon):
Both act on GABA-A receptors. Concurrent use provides no clinical benefit and increases risk of complex sleep behaviors, respiratory depression, and next-day impairment.
