PHARMACOLOGY: METHAMPHETAMINE

Identifiers: RxCUI=6816, ChEMBL=CHEMBL1201201, PubChem CID=10836
Molecular: C10H15N, MW=149.23
Brand Name: Desoxyn (rarely prescribed)
DEA Schedule: II

Methamphetamine is a potent synthetic amphetamine with high abuse potential. It is the most commonly used illicit stimulant worldwide and a major contributor to the "fourth wave" of the US drug overdose crisis (stimulant-involved deaths, often combined with fentanyl).

Mechanism:
  - Potent releasing agent at DAT, NET, and SERT (reverses transporter function)
  - VMAT2 inhibitor (disrupts vesicular monoamine storage)
  - Weak MAO inhibitor (slows monoamine degradation)
  - Net effect: massive dopamine efflux (10x greater than amphetamine per unit dose)

Pharmacokinetics:
  Onset: smoked/IV — seconds; oral — 30-60 minutes; intranasal — 3-5 minutes
  Half-life: 10-12 hours (much longer than cocaine, enabling prolonged binges)
  Duration: 8-24 hours depending on dose and route
  Metabolism: CYP2D6 (N-demethylation to amphetamine, 4-hydroxylation)

Neurotoxicity:
  - Dopaminergic terminal degeneration in striatum (DAT density reduced 20-30% after chronic use)
  - Reactive oxygen species generation from dopamine auto-oxidation
  - Mitochondrial dysfunction
  - Microglial activation and neuroinflammation
  - Gray matter volume loss (partially reversible with sustained abstinence)

Cardiovascular Toxicity:
  - Hypertension, tachycardia (sympathomimetic)
  - Methamphetamine-associated cardiomyopathy (dilated, often irreversible)
  - Aortic dissection, stroke (vasospasm + hypertension)
  - Pulmonary hypertension (especially with IV use)

Dental Toxicity:
  "Meth mouth": severe dental caries, periodontal disease, tooth fracture from xerostomia (dry mouth), bruxism, poor hygiene, and acidic oral environment.

Street Names: "ice", "crystal", "glass", "tina", "crank", "speed" (though "speed" can also refer to amphetamine), "tweak"

Opioid Interaction — "Goofball":
Methamphetamine + heroin/fentanyl ("goofball") is an increasingly common poly-drug pattern. Users report using methamphetamine to counteract opioid sedation or extend functionality while maintaining opioid dependence. This combination carries extreme cardiac and respiratory risk.
