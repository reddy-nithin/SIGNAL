ALCOHOL USE DISORDER TREATMENT

FDA-Approved Medications:

Naltrexone (oral 50 mg/day or IM depot 380 mg/month):
  Mechanism: mu-opioid receptor antagonist. Blocks ethanol-induced beta-endorphin release, reducing the rewarding effects of drinking.
  Efficacy: reduces heavy drinking days by approximately 25%, NNT (number needed to treat) ~12 for preventing return to any drinking.
  Key consideration: contraindicated in patients currently on opioid therapy or in acute opioid withdrawal. Must be opioid-free for 7-10 days before initiation.
  Relevance to SIGNAL: naltrexone mention in social media posts is a RECOVERY indicator, not a substance misuse signal.

Acamprosate (666 mg three times daily):
  Mechanism: modulates NMDA glutamate receptors and GABA-A receptors, partially normalizing the hyperexcitable state of the post-alcoholic brain.
  Efficacy: increases abstinence rates, most effective in maintaining abstinence (vs naltrexone which reduces heavy drinking).
  Key consideration: renally eliminated, dose-adjust for renal impairment. Does not interact with opioids.

Disulfiram (250 mg/day):
  Mechanism: irreversible ALDH2 inhibitor. Causes accumulation of acetaldehyde upon alcohol ingestion, producing severe nausea, flushing, tachycardia, hypotension (disulfiram-ethanol reaction).
  Efficacy: effective only when supervised administration is ensured (compliance is the main limitation).
  Key consideration: hepatotoxicity risk, requires liver function monitoring. Social media mentions may indicate early Recovery stage.

Off-Label Medications:
  - Gabapentin: reduces craving and anxiety, especially in patients with concurrent withdrawal symptoms
  - Topiramate: reduces heavy drinking days, may assist with weight management
  - Baclofen: GABA-B agonist, some evidence for reducing craving in patients with liver disease

Non-Pharmacological Treatments:
  - Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): most evidence-based psychotherapy
  - Motivational Enhancement Therapy (MET): effective for ambivalent patients
  - 12-Step Facilitation: Alcoholics Anonymous integration
  - SMART Recovery: cognitive-behavioral self-management approach
  - Contingency Management: incentive-based abstinence reinforcement

Medical Detoxification:
Supervised withdrawal using benzodiazepine taper (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide, or lorazepam) is standard of care for moderate-to-severe alcohol withdrawal. Symptom-triggered dosing based on CIWA-Ar scores reduces total benzodiazepine exposure and duration of treatment.
