ALCOHOL WITHDRAWAL SYNDROME

Alcohol withdrawal is a medical emergency. It is one of very few substance withdrawals that can be directly fatal (alongside benzodiazepine withdrawal). Mortality from untreated delirium tremens is 15-40%.

Pathophysiology:
Chronic alcohol exposure downregulates GABA-A receptors and upregulates NMDA glutamate receptors (neuroadaptation). Upon cessation, the resulting imbalance causes CNS hyperexcitability: unopposed glutamate excitation with insufficient GABAergic inhibition. This produces the hallmark symptoms of autonomic instability, seizures, and delirium.

Timeline of Withdrawal:
  6-12 hours: tremor, anxiety, nausea, insomnia, tachycardia, diaphoresis
  12-24 hours: alcoholic hallucinosis (visual, auditory, tactile hallucinations with intact sensorium)
  24-48 hours: withdrawal seizures (generalized tonic-clonic, may cluster)
  48-96 hours: delirium tremens (DTs) — confusion, agitation, autonomic storm, fever, hallucinations, potentially fatal

Risk Factors for Severe Withdrawal:
  - History of previous withdrawal seizures or DTs
  - Heavy daily consumption (>8 drinks/day)
  - Duration of heavy use (>10 years)
  - Concurrent medical illness or malnutrition
  - Older age
  - Co-use of benzodiazepines (cross-tolerance complicates withdrawal)

Clinical Assessment — CIWA-Ar:
The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol (revised) scores 10 symptoms on a 0-7 scale. Total score guides treatment intensity:
  <10: mild withdrawal, may not require pharmacotherapy
  10-18: moderate withdrawal, consider benzodiazepine treatment
  >18: severe withdrawal, aggressive treatment required

Treatment:
  - Benzodiazepines: first-line pharmacotherapy (diazepam or chlordiazepoxide preferred for long half-life; lorazepam if hepatic impairment)
  - Thiamine: 100-500 mg IV before glucose to prevent Wernicke encephalopathy
  - Fluids and electrolytes: correct hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia
  - Phenobarbital: adjunct for refractory DTs
  - ICU admission for DTs with hemodynamic instability

Comparison to Opioid Withdrawal:
Opioid withdrawal, while extremely uncomfortable, is rarely directly fatal in healthy adults. Alcohol withdrawal can kill through seizures, cardiac arrhythmia, or autonomic collapse. This distinction is critical for clinical triage — alcohol dependence cessation requires medical supervision.
